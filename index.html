I am a Ph.D. Candidate (ABD) in STEM-designated Economics at Texas Tech University, specializing in macroeconomics, applied econometrics, and structural quantitative modeling. My research studies how households, firms, and regions adjust to energy, climate, health, and technological shocks.

Job Market Paper
Consumer Demand and Household Welfare under Large Energy Price Shocks: Evidence and Structural Interpretation
Macroeconomics · Energy Economics · Consumption Dynamics
This paper examines whether consumption smoothing, a central implication of the Life-Cycle/Permanent-Income Hypothesis (LCPIH), remains stable during large modern oil-price shocks. Using U.S. data and exploiting the major oil-price episodes of 2008, 2020, and 2022, I identify household consumption responses through an instrumental-variables framework complemented by local projections and Bayesian VAR analysis. The evidence indicates that consumption dynamics are strongly state-dependent. Nondurable consumption remains broadly consistent with LCPIH predictions, whereas durable consumption becomes significantly excess-sensitive during oil-shock periods, implying a substantial breakdown in consumption smoothing.

To interpret these findings, I develop a heterogeneous-household structural framework featuring Ricardian and liquidity-constrained households, household energy expenditures, oil-intensive production, and external trade linkages. The model explains how oil-price shocks propagate simultaneously through household expenditure, production-cost, and trade-income channels, amplifying durable-consumption adjustment and welfare losses when financial constraints bind.

These findings remain robust across alternative oil-shock thresholds, oil-price measures, and historical episode definitions.

Methods: IV-GMM · Local Projections · Bayesian VAR · Heterogeneous-Household DSGE · GTAP Counterfactual Analysis

Job Market Paper
Consumer Demand and Household Welfare under Large Energy Price Shocks: Evidence and Structural Interpretation
Job Market Paper · Macroeconomics · Energy Economics · Household Finance · DSGE
Methods: IV-GMM · Local Projections · Bayesian VAR · Heterogeneous-Household DSGE · GTAP Counterfactual Analysis
  • Examines how large oil-price shocks disrupt household consumption smoothing, welfare, and economic resilience under borrowing and adjustment frictions.
  • Estimates dynamic adjustment across durable, nondurable, and total consumption during the 2008 oil spike, 2020 collapse, and 2022 Ukraine energy shock.
  • Combines causal evidence from historical energy shocks with a heterogeneous-household DSGE framework to quantify consumption responses, welfare losses, and policy effectiveness.
  • Maps household adjustment failures into trade-related welfare effects using GTAP counterfactual analysis.

U.S. Wildfire Exposure, Local Labor Adjustment, and Spatial Economic Resilience
Working Paper · Environmental Economics · Labor Economics · Regional Resilience

Selected Policy Research in Progress
Medicare Eligibility and Household Economic Resilience
Health Economics · Public Economics · Policy Evaluation
  • Uses a fuzzy regression discontinuity design and PSID data from 2013–2023 to identify Medicare's causal effects on healthcare utilization, spending, debt, labor supply, and financial resilience.
  • Examines how public insurance access affects consumption smoothing, economic vulnerability, and retirement behavior around the Medicare eligibility threshold.

An Agent-Based LLM Framework for Inflation Forecasting: Evidence from Federal Reserve Communications
Monetary Economics · AI Economics · Forecasting · Text-as-Data
  • Develops an agent-based New Keynesian model in which households, firms, and policymakers form expectations using macroeconomic indicators and Federal Reserve communications.
  • Integrates LLM-based expectation operators with text-derived signals extracted from FOMC statements using FinBERT and text-as-data methods.
  • Constructs a real-time information set combining inflation, unemployment, industrial production, interest rates, and central-bank communication.
  • Preliminary results indicate that communication-augmented forecasts outperform random-walk and autoregressive benchmarks in predicting U.S. inflation.

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy emphasizes economic intuition, analytical rigor, and evidence-based policy analysis. I guide students from economic intuition to formal models and empirical evidence using contemporary topics such as inflation, energy markets, artificial intelligence, climate policy, and international trade.

Across undergraduate and graduate courses, I emphasize causal reasoning, quantitative methods, reproducible research, and effective economic communication. Students learn not only economic theory but also how to apply modern econometric and computational tools to answer policy questions.

I integrate real-world datasets, Python, R, FRED, and AI-assisted data analysis into classroom instruction to prepare students for graduate study, research, government service, and industry careers.
