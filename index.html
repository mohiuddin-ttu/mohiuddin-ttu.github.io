Texas Tech University

I am a Ph.D. Candidate (ABD) in Economics at Texas Tech University, specializing in quantitative macroeconomics and structural econometrics. My research studies how economic agents make dynamic decisions under uncertainty, with a focus on consumption behavior, labor adjustment, and exposure to risk across different shock environments.

My current work investigates the welfare implications of oil-price shocks, wildfire risk and labor markets, AI exposure in U.S. trade, and the role of digitalization in public services. I combine reduced-form empirical methods with structural modeling to connect household decision rules to aggregate outcomes.

Job Market Paper
Price Shocks, Consumption Smoothing, and Household Welfare: State-Dependent Evidence on Intertemporal Adjustment
Macroeconomics · Energy Economics · Consumption Dynamics
The paper revisits the LCPIH using the 2008 oil-price spike, the 2020 pandemic collapse, and the 2022 Ukraine-driven surge. Consumption smoothing is state dependent: nondurable spending remains comparatively smooth, while durable spending contracts sharply when oil shocks interact with tighter household balance-sheet conditions. Using reduced-form oil-shock regressions, IV-GMM, local projections, and Bayesian VARs, the paper shows that the LCPIH excess-sensitivity parameter rises during modern oil-shock episodes and that implied welfare costs are economically large.

Teaching

Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in conceptual clarity, analytical discipline, and real-world application. I begin with intuition, move to formal analysis, and then connect the concept to data or a current policy problem.

Whether teaching introductory macroeconomics or econometrics, I emphasize how households, firms, and policymakers respond to shocks, constraints, and incentives.
